Texas Radiation Online- History of RadWaste Disposal in Texas Prelude to Sierra Blanca
In 1980, US Congress passed the Low-Level Radioactive Waste Policy Act (LLRWPA), (Public Law 96-573) (42 U.S.C. §§ 2021b-2021j), which directed that states form multistate compacts to develop regional disposal sites for "low-level" waste. This decision was emphasized in 1985 when Congress amended the LLRWPA in 1985 with the Low Level Radioactive Waste Policy Amendments Act (Public Law 99-240). Texas lawmakers had created the Low-Level Radioactive Waste Disposal Authority (LLRWDA) in 1981 to develop and operate a facility, or as one official put it, to seek "the best darn landfill man has ever seen." The Texas Compact itself was not ratified by US Congress until early 1998 during the massive effort to burden Sierra Blanca as a dump site.

There was also a concern in the mid-1980's about a possible high-level waste dump in Texas. The DOE's search for a high-level "geologic repository" was underway and Deaf Smith County had passed the first round of screenings, along with Nevada, Utah, New Mexico, Washington, and Mississippi. This later was decided to be sited at Yucca Mountain.

Originally, the Texas waste site was to have been south of San Antonio on privately owned land, but a law was passed mandating the site be located on state-owned land which eliminated virtually all sites in central, south, and east Texas. (AP 1991) The LLRWDA spent several years seeking possible sites for a commercial disposal facility, starting with statewide screenings including Dimmit and Deaf Smith counties, and attempting to place sites in Dell City, Hudspeth Co. in 1983-84; McMullen County in 1985-87; and in Ft Hancock, Hudspeth Co. from 1987-91.

These attempts to site a waste dump were abandoned due to local opposition and geologic unsuitability. For example, in the case of Fort Hancock, data had already been presented at TLLRWDA briefings in 1986 that the cretaceous aquifer underneath was vulnerable, as well as drainage to the Hueco Bolson and Rio Grande Alluvium aquifer systems, which the town of Fort Hancock used as a water supply. It was stated that run-off from the site also clearly flowed into the Rio Grande drainage basin,and it was recommended that the siting be dismissed. This was not deemed to be important by the TLLRWDA and they pushed forward with their objective.

After the TLLRWDA selected Fort Hancock as the preferred site in 1989, neighboring El Paso County and other parties challenged the site in a lawsuit. In January 1991, State District Judge William Moody declared the site unsuitable and ordered work on it stopped. After nearly a decade of being consistently stopped by public opposition, the TLLRWDA and it's supporters were getting impatient. Although previously rejected by the agency, the TLLRWDA returned to Sierra Blanca, ignoring the geologic unsuitability they had found in a manner quite similar to Fort Hancock.

The Sierra Blanca State Compact Dump (1991-1998)
The 72nd Legislature directed the TLLRWDA to select another disposal site within a 400-square-mile designated area in Hudspeth County (this designation was later nicknamed "The Box"). The authority chose a site known as Faskin Ranch about five miles southeast of Sierra Blanca and applied to TNRCC for a waste disposal facility license in 1992. In 1983, when the agency had hired an engineering consultant group to evaluate sites across the state, it had stated that the land containing the Sierra Blanca site was unsuitable. Despite this, the TLLRWDA said that it did not have complete environmental studies of the site before targeting it, and that only after purchasing the land did the agency realize that the site was located directly above an earthquake fault. Like in the Fort Hancock case, the TLLRWDA pushed forward with the site anyway, despite the data on site suitability.

Sierra Blanca is 76 miles southeast of El Paso, and 16 miles from Mexico and the Rio Grande. Even though it is the county seat of Hudspeth County, it didn't have its own sewage system or more than a few paved streets. Of the nearly 800 people who live there, 40% live in poverty, and 67% are Mexican American. 65 earthquakes over a 3.0 magnitude had occured within 200 miles of Sierra Blanca the previous 70 years. High rates of erosion were prevalent when digging, due to the desert winds and sandy soil. The 100-year floodplain of nearby Grayton Lake was adjacent to the site, and undergound fractures and faults promised a pathway for contamination to infiltrate the groundwater. In 1997, the City of El Paso had bought land near the site from which they hoped to pump El Paso's future drinking water.

[MERCO Joint Ventures and Texas Tech University had just recently obtained a permit to build what turned into the largest sewage dump in the world at Sierra Blanca, recieving 250 tons of "partially treated" New York City sewage sludge by rail each week. The site supposedly produced fertilizer, or "biosolids" out of the sludge by spraying the toxic slurry of heavy metals over the land to dry. This operation continued for nearly 10 years, assaulting the health of the residents and testing their tolerance for the noxious odors. The sludge site was licensed in a record 23 days, with the help of former legislator and former state water board commissioner Cliff Johnson (Johnson will resurface later as a key lobbyist for radioactive waste dumpers WCS, and Texas Tech's Chancellor John Montford would be one of the senators who helped WCS's Ken Bigham get his first legislative effort through the Senate in 1995).]

When Governor Ann Richards signed legislation consenting to join the Maine-Vermont Compact in 1993, the Sierra Blanca site was immediately designated as the host site. The compact had been delayed due to public opposition, but now passed, co-sponsored by 22 members of the House with Republicans Joe Barton and Tom DeLay leading a bi-partisan rally. By the time the US Congress was voting on enacting the compact in 1998, the opposition to the dump had been able to enlist the help of some sympathetic members of congress, such as Paul Wellstone (D-MN). Wellstone protested the compact strongly, raising the environmental issues, but also stressing the irony of the vast economic differences between the average incomes of the residents of Sierra Blanca, and those of the state of Maine.

The Sierra Blanca dump had gathered the most attention than any other disposal effort so far. Over the years, opposition grew from across the state, and after the Compact had passed, across the nation. Twenty US and Mexican governmental entities, environmental organizations and individuals participated in public hearings and legally challenged the draft operating license issued by the TNRCC in April 1996. In addition to the geological and socioeconomic considerations, building a waste disposal facility on the site threatened to violate the 1983 U.S.-Mexico La Paz agreement to keep polluting facilities 100 km or more away from the border. When the Mexican Congress openly began to reconsider NAFTA, Washington began to take notice.

There were many other key actions, including:
- children (aged kindergarten to middleschool) from a neighboring community in Mexico participated in blockading the international bridge at Del Rio-Ciudad Acuna in protest, and at a later date also in protest at the Texas capitol in Austin.
- Mexican officials camped out at the capitol in protest
- In early August 1998, 500 Mexicans and Texans participated in a 4-day, 76-mile march to Sierra Blanca from El Paso- Ciudad Juarez
- Bill Addington, a resident of Sierra Blanca who founded the Sierra Blanca Legal Defense Fund, fasted for 56 days in protest of the dump.

In October 1998, after 2 years of meetings, the State Office of Administrative Hearings (SOAH) had finished a contested case hearing on the license application. SOAH judges recommended denying the license saying, "based on the [TLLRWDA's] failure to adequately characterize the fault beneath the site, and failure to adequately address potential negative socioeconomic impacts from the proposed facility" A decision was then left to the TNRCC commissioners. At the scheduled meeting two months later, several hundred people camped outside in the TNRCC parking lot including Mexican congressmen and other officials, activists from Mexico and across Texas. Entertainers and the press corp joined them in the large gathering which continued throughout the night after the decision. The TNRCC commissioners capitulated and cancelled the license application.

After spending over $12 million in 18 years, the TLLRWDA still did not have their radioactive waste dump and only reaped public opposition. The Sierra Blanca site would be their last attempt, as the TLLRWDA would be dissolved and incorporated into the TNRCC during the 76th legislature. WCS and Envirocare had been battling it out in the background for some time to license a private dump for themselves, and Texas still did not have a disposal facility for the radioactive waste compact, and the competing obstacle of the TLLRWDA was now out of the picture.

Maine and Vermont kept their $25 million, the box remained in Hudspeth County, and Texans won a bit of time and postponed the Compact dump for a little while longer. However, WCS found ways to start dumping some DOE waste through an exemption loophole, and future legislatures would consider the ramifications of either contracting a private company under a state-held license or allowing a private company to hold the license, which both risk opening the state to massive amounts of DOE waste. The question for many waste dump supporting lawmakers at this point was either "will the compact dump be placed in Ward County or Andrews County?" Regardless of where in Texas a dump would be placed, Texas law dictates that a company can evade reponsibility for the waste by filing bankruptsy and transferring title/ ownership to the State of Texas [Health and Safety Code, §401.205(3)].

Concurrent Texas Waste Wars
As the battle over the Sierra Blanca dump was being won in the late 1990s, two waste management companies were battling it out, competing to become blessed with a license for a Texas dump. It got so bad that supporters of the Texas Compact were afraid that the activity would jeopardize the Sierra Blanca dump. It was even then suggested in WCS-backed legislative amendments that the compact dump be moved to Andrews. Since the defeat of the Sierra Blanca dump, this idea has remained a part of WCS's legal push.

Envirocare, which is licensed in Utah for a major national waste dump, was attempting to place a dump on its land near Barstow in Ward County, and was not preparing to surrender to a potential new competitor. October of 1996, a major bribery dispute occured when the former Director of the Utah Bureau of Radiation Control Larry Anderson sued Envirocare's owner, Khosrow Semnani, complaining that he had stopped paying him hundreds of thousands of dollars in "consulting fees" for work done while he was still Director of the Bureau. Semnani countersued accusing the ex-regulator of extortion. It was found that Semnani had already given Anderson $600,000 in cash, gold coins, and real estate. The case obviously didn't help Envirocare much, and the company's opponents took full advantage of the situation, with allegations that they had "bought" their license. Semnani was fined $100,000, and Anderson was charged for mail fraud, tax fraud, and extortion. The DOE demanded that the owner step down, and kept Envirocare as a client. Envirocare had also contributed over $90,000 to Utah legislators in the previous election cycle, including $50,000 to Governor Leavitt's campaign.

Later on, Envirocare left Texas as part of an agreement, not only under pressure of the company rivalry, but also the public opposition to their dump in related counties. This relieved tensions in not only Ward but also Loving County, where Envirocare owned land targeted as a potential dump. This relief proved to be only temporary, as much of this land was later sold to WCS.

The second company, Waste Control Specialists (aka WCS, then run out of Pasadena by Ken Bigham), had land in Andrews County upon for which it had held a hazardous waste dump license since 1994, and by this time already had quite a reputation around the State Capitol for aggressive lobbying and several incidents of alleged "improper and unethical" conduct. In 1995, WCS lobbyists unsuccessfully attempted to convert an outspoken republican critic of their bill with a promise of $60,000 in campaign contributions, which backfired. Later that year in November, 50% of WCS was bought by Dallas-based Valhi Inc., run by billionaire Harold Simmons, who is a personal friend of George W. Bush and major contributor to his campaigns. Simmons had a reputation for funnelling massive campaign contributions to state and federal candidates, and had an team of high paid Washington lobbyists to orchestrate these Congressional allies when elected.

The lobbying team for WCS has been made up almost exclusively of ex-state officials and congressmen. Leading up the team was Kent Hance, former Railroad Commissioner, who represented Andrews County when he was a Congressman in the 1980s. Hance was a WCS major stockholder and Chairman with an option to buy 25% of the company. Carl Parker is a former state senator (D-Port Arthur); Chet Brooks, a former state senator; Cliff Johnson, former state representative from Palestine who served as commissioner of the Texas Water Board; Jim Rudd, former state rep from Brownfield; Daryl Owen, former top aide to the senate energy committee.

At a legislative agenda meeting of El Paso city, county, and state officials held December 1996, a resolution opposing the Sierra Blanca site was unanimously approved. According to those present, only one official wanted to request specifically that the compact dump be moved to Andrews County: El Paso state representative Pat Haggerty. Ethics Commission filings show that Haggerty received campaign donations from Ken Bigham, and WCS lobbyists Chet Brooks and Carl Parker - all on the same day, and only a few weeks prior to the El Paso meeting.

By early 1997, WCS had submitted an application and soon afterward was granted a license to process and store, but not dispose radioactive waste at Andrews. This and the 75th legislature occured in the midst of a 20-month lawsuit between WCS and the DOE. The Dallas Morning News obtained court documents after the dismissal of the WCS vs DOE lawsuit in 1998 which revealed a number of things about WCS's character. In line with the Simmons-Valhi reputation, WCS had been hiring well-connected lobbyists in Washington, for example, former top Senate Energy Committee aide Daryl Owen had been lobbying for WCS since October 1995, and was promised a huge $18.4 million award if successful. By this time, Simmons, Bigham and others connected to WCS had already donated over $90,000 to key senators and House members.

Also revealed was that Kent Hance had asked three senators who had recieved large WCS contributions to block the nomination of Mary Anne Sullivan for DOE General Counsel, which succeeded [the three were Shelby (R-Alaska), Hutchison (R-Texas) and Gramm (R-Texas)]. WCS was even getting its congressional allies to assist in things like letterwriting campaigns. One targeted at Energy Secretary Federico Pena asked him why his department was still doing business with Envirocare, and urging him to reconsider WCS proposals during the WCS vs DOE lawsuit. Gramm, Hutchison, and Shelby were among the letterwriters, which included one letter signed by 18 of the 30 Texas House members, nine of whom had received donations from top WCS supporters.

Wright Andrews, past president of the American League of Lobbyists, said WCS's lobbying has been hard-edged, even for Washington. "It sounds as thought this has been an exceptionally aggressive and very political lobbying effort." He continued that he had lobbied Congress on nuclear waste issues before, "but it sounds like it is high-stakes"

In February 1997, WCS influence was clear at a hearing of the House Appropriations Committee, as Reps Pat Haggerty and Talmadge Heflin, supported by Sierra Blanca Representative Pete Gallego, went after the TLLRWDA's funding. The agency's director, Rick Jacobi was overwhelmed by questions about the agency's bloated budget and questionable past expenditures, the Authority faced a weary committee grown unanimously impatient with the issue. "They've expended a tremendous amount of time and resources," said committee chair Rob Junell (who also received a $1,000 contribution from Simmons the previous year), "and they don't have much to show for it." Haggerty calculated that the agency had already spent $12 million on legal fees for only nine days' worth of actual hearings. The committee voted unanimously to defund the Authority.

It wasn't until the next legislative session, however, that the TLLRWDA was dissolved in 1999 by HB 2954. The bill was originally submitted by Gray, and was later hijacked by Buster Brown in the closing days of the Session when the other major bills relating to radioactive materials had died. Rather than simply abolish the Authority as the bill prescribed, other legislators added an amendment to transfer it's functions to the TNRCC. Two other major bills during the 76th session were submitted by Representative Chisum. HB 1910, as released from the House Environmental Regulation Committee, would have authorized a facility to bury the waste underground or store it in above-ground containers with the intent of long-term management or disposal, and it would have prohibited issuing the license to a private entity. However, the prohibition against licensing a private entity was removed during House floor debate, and the bill died in the Senate Natural Resources Committee. HB 1171 as amended by the Senate, would have allowed TNRCC to issue a disposal license to a private entity, but the bill died late in the session when the House did not consider the Senate amendments.

During this legislative session, HB 1171 had been amended to introduce a new concept known as assured isolation. As discussed in the section on assured isolation, this concept was (and still is) poorly outlined and not specifically defined by federal bodies. There is no federal law which outlines the concept, and since Texas is required to model its laws regulating radioactive materials on the federal laws and NRC practices, this legislation arguably attempts to overstep the federal lead.

The cornerstone of assured isolation is the idea that the facility will be a storage site for waste for 300-500 years. When this time is up, the decision will be made as to whether the site should be decommissioned and cleaned up (sending the waste to another place for dumping), or to just convert the place into a waste dump itself. This postponement of whether to call the site a disposal facility or not was openly designed by the original authors to keep critics from calling the place a radioactive waste dump. In addition, cameras and giger counters are to be used to make up a rudamentary monitoring system labeled "enhanced monitoring." The presence of this monitoring has been used in just about every version of assured isolation to justify weak siting criteria, a lack of external monitoring, and even dismissing the standard environmental reports.

At the request of two state agencies (the Connecticut Hazardous Waste Management Service and the Texas Low-Level Radioactive Waste Disposal Authority), the DOE's Low-Level Waste Management Program has produced a few evaluations of the concept, yet has not developed a formal definition of assured isolation, and merely offered some additional descriptions, which provide the bulk of all federal reports on the issue. The DOE says that an Environmental Assessment is unnecessary and inappropriate, and has not conducted a study to produce an Environmental Impact Statement, which is normally required before such waste management methods are implemented.

These initial Texas attempts to legally define assured isolation were extremely primitive, and implemented a nonfunctional definition that the DOE had used, that "'assured isolation' means an integrated management system for isolating low-level radioactive waste through robust, accessible facilities, planned preventative maintenance, and sureties adequate to address contingencies or implement future management alternatives." Usage of the word "robust" in this manner can be traced back to the 1995 debut of the assured isolation concept, where the author states that the facility "will have many robust features, such as concrete building and overpacks." Obviously, robust is a bit of an overstatement for a simple concrete structure.

Assured isolation has been reintroduced in slightly modified forms in every legislative session since. More attention has been given to the financial needs of the idea, such as separate accounts for both options of conversion and decommissioning. Some variations on environmental criteria and monitoring have been made, but one thing remains glaringly obvious: the presence of extensive discussion of how to convert the site into a dump, but absolutely nothing outlining the implementation of the decommissioning option. For more information on assured isolation, please see the section devoted to it here.

Since WCS had been granted a radioactive materials processing and storage license from the Texas Bureau of Radiation Control, it had been pushing its way as close to disposing without a disposal license as it could. Early on, it focused on accepting materials for disposal which the NRC exempted from being considered legally radioactive. This exempted material could be waste that had been diluted down, or was already at levels of radioactivity that would be considered "below regulatory concern." In this manner, WCS could sell their hazardous waste dump area as a de facto "low-level" waste disposal unit, and as a cheaper alternative to bona fide facilities appropriately licensed for radioactive waste disposal. In addition to this, WCS has been asking for amendments to its processing and storage license at a rate of 8 to 12 attempts per year, one in particular attempting to exempt americium-241 waste, which is by rule not to be exempted. Pushing the envelope of their license by asking for these add-on amendments has been assigned a slang term: "license creep."

During the DOE vs WCS lawsuit, Attorney General Dan Morales made the statement that WCS was dictating regulatory policy to the agencies responsible for oversight and regulation. This just so happens to not be isolated to the DOE. It is not only apparent in the documents WCS submits as proposals to the Bureau of Radiation Control, but by the company's behaviour in meetings with the Bureau. Throughout the submitted written proposals is a clear tone of a demanding presumption of will, and not one of respectfully requesting for the Bureau's permission.

During the 77th Legislature, Sen. Robert Duncan produced SB 1541, and Representative Chisum produced the House equivalent, HB 3420. This bill would have allowed TNRCC to issue a license to a private entity for a radioactive waste disposal or above-ground isolation facility. The Senate Natural Resources Committee’s substitute version would have authorized the license holder explicitly to dispose of noncompact DOE waste. This committee substitute version amounted to a highjacking of Sen. Duncan's bill by Sen. Bivins, the familiar champion of private disposal for WCS. SB 1541 passed the Senate and, as substituted by the House Environmental Regulation Committee, would have tightened restrictions on the license holder’s acceptance of federal waste. The bill died in the closing hours of the House Calendars Committee. Right in the middle of the session, Sen Buster Brown was caught with his hands in the waste disposal cookie jar when receiving a $10,000 "gift" from WCS's Harold Simmons.

"The DOE has identified this case for what it really is, an effort to dictate waste management policy to the agency (and the nation) that, not coincidentally, would offer WCS a way to evade Texas state law and serve the firm's economic interests." Texas Attorney General Dan Morales, Brief for Amicus Curiae State of Texas, March 1998.

The DOE v.s WCS Lawsuit (Sept 1996-May 1998) : Bypassing State Law
Envirocare's Utah site is the nation's only state-licensed private low-level waste dump, and has been the source of much aggressive rivalry with WCS. During a lawsuit with the DOE, WCS claimed that Envirocare had monopoly status on dumping DOE waste, and that WCS should also be able to open a DOE dump in Texas and reap the promised billions in profits. Before WCS delivered its proposal to the DOE or the TNRCC in the case, Envirocare Chief Executive Khosrow Semnani bought up 880 acres near the WCS site and notified the TNRCC of an intent to license a federal DOE dump which served as a pre-emptive strike against WCS, forcing a TNRCC policy decision before WCS could act.

Since WCS was unable to change Texas law in 1995 to allow a private company a license for a dump, they prepared a method to bypass state law. In September 1996, WCS submitted a bid in response to a DOE open call for proposals to take waste from it's plant in Fernald, Ohio. Part of the deal was that the company must take title to the waste, and be able to get the proper disposal permits. The DOE did not see WCS as qualifying, and just about the same time on October 18, TNRCC denied Envirocare's request for a license, saying it didnt have the legal authority to grant such a permit under state law. TNRCC executive director Dan Pearson sent a letter to WCS saying the same thing, and emphasized that as a matter of policy the agency was opposed to "any scenario or arrangement" that involved state oversight of a private disposal facility.

In a second letter, Pearson suggested that a federally regulated DOE site was a legal feasibility, and if such a proposal were formally made it would merely require a policy decision by the TNRCC's three commissioners, who were all Bush appointees. WCS's response was to submit another proposal to the DOE which outlined how they could operate a dump legally under DOE contract without obtaining a state or NRC license. This also involved a research partnership with Texas Tech University. They had hoped that if the DOE opened a site with them at Andrews, that this would exclude the State of Texas from having any say in the process, for they claimed that as a DOE contracted site, they should be exempted from State regulation. Citing internal policy, the DOE said they couldn't do it, so WCS filed a complaint saying its proposal could be lawfully implemented and that DOE unfairly rejected the proposal. DOE responded that they did see what WCS proposed as being lawful, but that alternative regulatory agreements such as suggested were not covered under a poilicy decicion.

On October 3, 1997, Judge Joe Kendall of the U.S. District Court for the Northern District of Texas, Wichita Falls Division, heard WCS' case against DOE, and issued a preliminary injunction against DOE. He determines that a state and/or NRC permit is not necessary for DOE radioactive waste disposal at a privately-owned site under contract with DOE. The injunction barred DOE from awarding contracts for the disposal of low-level and/or mixed radioactive wastes without considering bids from WCS.

By February 1998 the lawsuit sped up when it came to the attention of the National Governors' Association, who weren't too pleased that the DOE considered the WCS proposal as legal (since it infringed on state rights). WCS started asking for injunctive reliefs such as: preventing the DOE from using U.S. Army Corps of Engineers contracts to ship their low-level wastes to Envirocare's private dump in Utah; and, keeping the DOE from shipping low-level wastes to states unless an agreement existed with a particular state. Two days later, sixteen states responded very concerned, with an amicus brief of a full statement condemning WCS's arguments.

Among other things they said, "if a privately owned and operated facility that seeks to profit from disposing radioactive waste generated by DOE is exempt from external regulation, public health and the environment may be severely compromised. Radiation protection regulations must not be rendered ineffective merely because a private facility agrees to dispose DOE waste." They continued that "the WCS proposal cannot be implemented under the Atomic Energy Act. WCS is not exempt from State regulation simply because it disposes DOE waste at its privately owned and operated facility. Further, disposal of DOE waste at the WCS facility in the absence of State regulation is inconsistent with the Low-Level Radioactive Waste Policy Amendments Act of 1985."

U.S. 5th Circuit Court of Appeals reversed the preliminary injunctions placed upon the DOE and dismissed the case in May 1998. However, the court not only denied entering the amicus brief the states had filed but also found that the district court was correct in determining that a state or NRC license is not required for a private site to dispose of low-level or mixed radioactive waste from the DOE, but that the DOE has the discretion to require a state or NRC license before granting a contract.

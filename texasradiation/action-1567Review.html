July 20, 2005- TCEQ issued WCS a courtesy letter with early notification of issues that are of fundamental importance to the consideration of the WCS application.

In 2003, the Texas State Legislature considered a bill which it had been presented with in several different forms for many years, and finally signed it into law. Just like previous versions, that session's version of the bill (known as House Bill 1567 aka HB1567) was largely written by a group of extremely highly paid lobbyists for the waste disposal industry, specifically a company in Andrews County called Waste Control Specialists LLC (WCS). The company had accomplished this through years of pouring millions of dollars in campaign contributions and soft-money contributions to state legislators.

[Note: this issue has alot of prehistory associated with it. See the sections on WCS in Texas and Waste Disposal in Texas for detailed background. Another important factor to waste disposal in Texas is an agreement known as "The Texas Compact" which states that Texas is to be responsible for taking radioactive waste from nuclear power plants in Maine and Vermont, and is outlined in its own section.]

One of the things that the new law did was to allow companies in Texas to import nuclear waste which was not the responsibility of the State itself, which was previously prevented (with the exception of waste from the Texas Compact). It also went even further and outlined how the designated company would import huge amounts of waste from nuclear weapons factories operated by the US Department of Energy. This was unprecedented- previous bills had merely removed the prohibition on importation, while HB1567 specifically mandated a second waste dump for this federal nuclear waste. The bill masqueraded as limiting the size of the dumps, with specified limits, yet merely accomodated liberal amounts of waste which experts had discussed as what could be feasibly imported at the site over the specified time period anyway.

The new law presented a regulatory timeline, which along with its other contents, was inserted into the regulatory body's rulebook later in the year. During this rulemaking process, dissent had limited scope, since this agency was operating under legal mandate, from which it had no choice. The agency delegated to regulate waste disposal in Texas is the Texas Commission on Environmental Quality, (the TCEQ, Texas EPA, previously TNRCC). In written comments for this rulemaking, WCS complained that HB1567 didn't go far enough, and that limiting the amount of waste they could import was somehow unusually restrictive.

As it passed and was signed into law, this bill:
            - Mandated that a private corporation will operate 2 nuclear dumps in Texas. This replaced a State prohibition on allowing a private company to become licensed for radioactive waste disposal.
            - Two dumps at one site: One dump is for the Texas Compact (commercial power plant waste), and an separate dump right next door for Federal waste (from nuclear weapons facilities). Both dumps are to open at the same time, and both share the same license.
            - Importation of 162 million cubic feet of nuclear weapons-related federal waste: 3 million cubic yards for the first 5 years (81 million cubic feet), and then doubled to 6 million cubic yards (162 million cubic feet) of radioactive waste. Federal waste amount equates to a football field 282 (281.74) stories tall* - 60 times what the Texas Compact was estimated to bring to Sierra Blanca over 35 years! 10% of federal waste can be Class B and C (see waste types overview for definitions)
            [*Department of Energy, EM Progress, Fall/Winter 2002, pg 9. Stated the Nevada Test site recieved 2.3 million cubic feet of waste the previous year, and said that amount "equates" to a football field 4 stories tall.]
            - Miniscule Insurance Coverage: The company only has to give $20 million to Texas for liability coverage. Host County (presumably) gets 5% of gross reciepts for public spending, another 5% goes into a perpetual care fund. In the bill's fiscal report, 5% of reciepts is projected as being $400,000 per year (at $8 million yearly gross reciepts). After 10 years, this amounts to $4 million, which would be placed into the State's Perpetual Care Account. Added to the $20 million in financial security to cover the cost of leakage, etc., This $24 million is a small fraction of the hundreds of millions of dollars (if not several billion) which is required to clean up leaking sites of this sort.
